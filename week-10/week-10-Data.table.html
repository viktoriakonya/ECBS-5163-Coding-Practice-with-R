Data.table

We will look at data.table package.

For this the first step is to transfer the data frame to a data.table format. For this you can use as.data.table(), data.table() or setDF(df).

Note that the first two have to be assigned back to a new variable, while setDF() does it inplace.

dt <- data.table(df)

Alternatively you can read your data with fread() instead of read.csv() or read_csv(). It is cool as it is 20x faster than the simple read ones.

class(df)
## [1] "data.frame"
class(dt)
## [1] "data.table" "data.frame"

Data.table aims to reach similar outcomes. The three parts we will be covering are:

  • Filter rows
  • Select or calculate columns
  • Group by

Filter rows

You can filter rows by their number. This gives the first 5 rows of the df. For a data frame the same command will filter for the first 5 columns!

dim(df[1:5])
## [1] 250   5
dim(dt[1:5])
## [1]  5 30

You can add a , after the number to make sure you filter for rows. If you do so for a data frame that is not a data.table, this makes sure that you filter for rows, if you do not include, it will filter for columns.

dim(df[1:5])
## [1] 250   5
dim(df[1:5,])
## [1]  5 30

The power of data.table lies in the ease of filtering and arranging and calculating new values.

Select or calculate columns

df[filter, select or calculate,...]

As the second variable for in the [] you can select given columns from the data.

dt[, title][1:5] 
## [1] "The Shawshank Redemption" "The Godfather"           
## [3] "The Godfather: Part II"   "The Dark Knight"         
## [5] "12 Angry Men"

This will return with a vector.

You have to use list() or .() to return with a data.table.

dt[, .(title)][1:5]
##                       title
## 1: The Shawshank Redemption
## 2:            The Godfather
## 3:   The Godfather: Part II
## 4:          The Dark Knight
## 5:             12 Angry Men

Group by

df[filter, select or calculate, group by]
